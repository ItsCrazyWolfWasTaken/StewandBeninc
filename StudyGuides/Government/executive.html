Executive Unit

      Qualifications for Office, Term Limits, & Disability

      Succession

    • Presidential Succession Act 1947: Establishes order of succesion. VP, Speaker of H.O.R., PPT, cabinet members 1-18. Any acting president can be bumped by someone higher in order of succesion.
    • 25th Amendment (proposed 1965; ratified 1967): When the President dies, resigns, or is removed from office the VP becomes president. The President has to fill the VP vacancy with Congress' approval. It also established a way for the presidental powers to transfer while the President is incapiatated.

      • Term Limits and Qualifactions

    • 22nd Amendment (proposed 1947; ratified 1951): The President can't hold the office for more than 2 terms.
    • Qualifactions for office: You have to be a natural-born citizen of the United States, be at least 35 years old, and have been a resident of the United States for 14 years.

      • 8 Roles of the President

    • Chief of State: The ceremonial head of the United States. The British monarch is the Chief of State in England, but they do not hold other roles.
    • Chief Executive: Holds the power of the executive branch
    • Chief Administrator: Head of the executive branch and its employees
    • Chief Diplomat: Head of Diplomacy in the U.S. and set foreign policy.
    • Chief Legislator: The Author of the Countries Policies. It is most often the President who set the shape of the congressional agenda
    • Chief Economist: Head of the nations economy and has to keep an eye on it and intervene when nessesary
    • Chief Citizen: Representive for the Country
    • Chief of Party: Head of the political party they represent

      Role of VP

    • Changing Role of the VP: They gained a more prominent role after WWII because the nuclear weapons command was needed at all times and the chance of death was higher. If the president were to die the VP needed to know everything to take over.
    • Balancing the Ticket: When the President picks someone of different ideological, geographic, racial, ehtnic, gender or other charecteristics.

      Domestic Powers of POTUS

      Theories

    • Whig Theory: The president should exist to execute the policies of Congress.
    • Stewardship Theory: The president should have all the powers not prohibited by the Constitution and act on personal initiative.

      • Powers

    • Executive Orders: Directives issued by the president to manage operations of the federal government, subject to judicial and congressional limits.
    • Executive Privilege: The right to withhold information from Congress, courts, or the public to protect national interests.
    • Appointments: The president's power to appoint federal officials, judges, and cabinet members, subject to Senate confirmation.
    • Removal Power: The ability to remove executive officials. The Tenure of Office Act (1867–1887) said that the President had to get the approval of the Senate first.
    • Power of Clemency: It is in four types: pardons, reprieves, commutations, and amnesty. Pardons are removal of the charge any point in the proccess (sentencing, procecution, in prison). Amnesty is a group pardon. Commutation is postponing the proccess and reprieve is reducing the sentence.
    • "Bully Pulpit": The president has the ability to call a press conference at any time and have total access to national media. His ability to change public opinion initiatives through media is a result of that and is called the bully pulpit.
    • Veto & Line-Item Veto: Vetoing is the president's power to reject legislation. line-item veto was ruled unconstitutional in 1998.
    • Signing Statements: Statements at the end of a signed bill written by the president indicating constitutional concerns or enforcement plans.

      Foreign Powers of POTUS

      Powers

    • Diplomatic Recognition: The president’s authority to formally recognize foreign governments and that they are on good terms.
    • Persona Non Grata: The power to recogonize a diplomat who is no longer welcome in the country.
    • Executive Agreements: Agreements with nations. Treaties that do not require Senate approval.
    • Diplomatic Immunity: Diplomats cannot be prosecuted in U.S.
    • Undeclared War: The president’s ability to engage military forces without a formal declaration of war, using the role of Commander-in-Chief.
